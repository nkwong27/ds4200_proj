Introduction

Technology stocks are an increasingly central pillar of the U.S. economy, with tech-based firms representing nearly 40 percent of the top 500 publicly traded companies and commanding trillion-dollar valuations. Given the sector's rapid expansion, particularly with the acceleration of artificial intelligence (AI) adoption, its influence on financial markets and the broader economy is growing yearly. This project aims to understand the relationship between macroeconomic conditions and the financial performance of major U.S. technology companies. By analyzing long-term economic trends alongside tech stock behavior, we seek to identify key relationships and gain insight into how broader economic changes influence the technology market over time.

Volatility Calendar Heatmap

Monthly volatility patterns across ticker selections.

Visualization Details

This interactive visualization displays the monthly volatility of each stock using the difference between the monthly high and low prices.

  • X-axis: Month (January through December)
  • Y-axis: Year, providing a long-term view of volatility patterns
  • Volatility Metric: Calculated using the High–Low spread for each month
  • Color Scale: Dark blue represents low volatility, while red represents high volatility
  • Interaction: Users can select any ticker from the dropdown menu, and hovering over each cell reveals the exact volatility value for that month
  • Purpose: The calendar layout makes it easy to observe seasonality, crisis-related spikes, and long-term volatility trends across companies

Key Takeaways

  • Many companies show decreasing volatility over time, indicated by increasing dark-blue regions in recent years.
  • The heatmap highlights major economic disruptions, such as the 2008 financial crisis, where volatility significantly increased across most stocks.
  • Certain companies exhibit seasonal volatility patterns, with specific months consistently showing higher turbulence.
  • Volatility patterns vary significantly across companies, demonstrating that market stability is not uniform across the tech sector.
  • The visualization suggests that volatility tends to rise during periods of economic uncertainty and stabilizes as conditions improve.

Macroeconomic Factors vs. Tech Stock Performance Plots

Macro indicator alignment vs tech performance over time.

Visualization Details

This interactive visualization displays 6 different macroeconomic indicator compared to average tech stock price over time.

  • X-axis: Date from 2000 to 2025, each month is a point
  • Y-axis: Tech sector average represents average stock price of all tech companies in S&P 500
  • Normalized Values: Values are normalized using min max normalization so that all y values are between 0 and 100, making it easier to compare when attributes are in the same units.
  • Interaction:There is hover data for every point, showing the exact value of the macroeconomic indicator and the average tech stock price for that month.

Key Findings

  • The Tech Sector Performance has shown a steady upward trend, especially in the most recent years where performance has exponentially grown.
  • In contrast the macroeconomic indicators generally show more volatility except Real GDP and Nominal GDP.
  • Real GDP and Nominal GDP have been significantly higher than tech stock performance until 2020, where the tech stock performance is nearly at the same level as GDP. This means the surge in tech could become the primary source of economic growth.
  • Unemployment Rates have seen larger and larger spikes more recently and Labor Force Participation has been steadily decreasing. These trends in employment are inversely related to the Tech Sector Performance, meaning tech stock performance does not guarantee labor market health which could be because tech is generating automation and newer technology that might be replacing or reducing the need for traditional labor.

Animated Bubble Chart

Market Cap Vs. Stock Prices Over Time By Company and Trading Volume.

Visualization Details

This interactive visualization displays tech stocks from the S&P 500 aggregated by subsector over time:

  • X-axis: Total market capitalization utilizing a log scale to display a larger range of values
  • Y-axis: Average stock price weighted by market cap
  • Bubbles: Each bubble represents an aggregation of tech stocks in the S&P 500 by 6 different subsectors, represented by color
  • Bubble Size: The size of the bubble represents the total trading volume
  • Animation Controls: There is a play and pause button to see the different tech sector stocks animated over time; if it is not animated, the month and year displayed at the bottom can be used as a slider (option for animation or static interactive display)
  • Hover Data: There's also hover data for more detailed information at each point of the animation

Key Findings

  • Over the first decade of the timeline, average stock prices across tech subsectors remain relatively stable, while total market capitalization dips slightly before recovering back to its initial level, indicating steady valuations despite a temporary industry-wide correction.
  • Semiconductors and Technology Hardware show the largest fluctuations in trading volume, reflecting sensitivity to supply-demand shifts, and strong investor reactions to market events
  • Communication Equipment, Electronic Equipment, and IT Services show minimal changes in trading volume and exhibit steady, modest increases in average stock price, suggesting these are stable, low-volatility subsectors.
  • Software demonstrates the strongest growth in average stock price, highlighting investor confidence in scalable, recurring-revenue business models and the sector's pivotal role in digital transformation.
  • Market capitalization grows the most for Software, Semiconductors, and Technology Hardware, indicating these subsectors drive the majority of economic value creation in the tech industry through innovation, demand growth, and market expansion.

Conclusion

Across event-aligned windows, the tech sector shows distinct crisis-era behavior, but the broader visualizations reveal deeper structural patterns. While the Dot-Com Bubble produced a sharp downturn and COVID-19 led to strong cumulative gains, the volatility heatmap shows that tech stocks remain consistently more volatile than most sectors even outside recessions. The bubble charts further highlight how the largest firms captured disproportionate market-cap share over time, especially during recoveries, suggesting a growing concentration of influence. Meanwhile, the macro-sensitivity faceted plots show that tech's response to shifts in interest rates, inflation, or GDP is far from uniform across firms, reinforcing that performance is shaped by both macro conditions and firm-specific dynamics. Together, these visuals point to a sector that does not simply react to economic cycles but increasingly shapes them, as rising concentration, persistent volatility, and heterogeneous macro sensitivity push tech toward becoming a driver of broader economic outcomes rather than a follower.

Future work could compare tech with other S&P 500 sectors to see whether its crisis performance is unique or simply more visible due to scale. Adding macro variables and predictive tools—machine learning, time-series models, and event-driven forecasting—would strengthen the analysis. This would move the project from describing market behavior to actively predicting it, helping determine whether tech is driving economic change or still being driven by it.
